Background In 2008, a “second-generation cut-off” rule was implemented, which limited citizenship by descent to the first generation born abroad pursuant to s. 3(3)(a) Citizenship Act. In practical terms, if a Canadian obtained their citizenship by descent while being born abroad, the amendment prohibited them passing on their citizenship to their child also born abroad. As per the Minister of Citizenship and Immigration at the time, the purpose of the law was to “protect the value of Canadian citizenship by ensuring that our citizens have a real connection to this country.” More specifically, the concern was with respect to successive generations of children born abroad being conferred citizenship by descent in perpetuity, without they or their parents ever having lived in Canada or having any substantial connection to Canada. Constitutional Court Challenge In 2023, s. 3(3)(a) of the Citizenship Act was challenged constitutionally by a group of Canadian citizens, specifically seven Canadian families, who were affected by this “second-generation cut-off.” On December 19, 2023, the Ontario Superior Court of Justice issued its decision finding s. 3(3)(a) of the Citizenship Act violates ss. 6 and 15 of the Canadian Charter of Rights and Freedoms (Charter). The full decision is available here: Bjorkquist et al. v. Attorney General of Canada The Court granted an order which exempted the applicants born abroad from s. 3(3)(a) and gave their children Canadian citizenship. Under s. 6 of the Charter, every Canadian has the right to “enter, remain in, and leave Canada.” Section 15 of the Charter guarantees Canadians equality under the law without discrimination based on race, national or ethnic origin, colour, religion, sex, age, or mental or physical disability. The Court concluded that s. 3(3)(a) creates a distinction based on national origin by treating Canadians born abroad differently from Canadians born in Canada. The Court found Canadians obtaining citizenship by descent because they were born outside of Canada “hold a lesser class of citizenship” because they do not have the automatic right to return to Canada to live with their born-abroad children. The applicants also argued that s. 3(3)(a) violated s. 15 “based on the intersection of national origin and sex” because of the provision’s disproportionate impact on women. The Court agreed and found that s. 3(3)(a) had a heightened impact on the female applicants because they were the ones who were pregnant and would be required to return to Canada to give birth if their children were to be born Canadian citizens. The judge found their becoming pregnant while in the midst of establishing their careers, as is common, exacerbated the differential impact because it made relocation more difficult. The Court held the law “disadvantages pregnant first-generation born abroad women who are living abroad when they get pregnant by placing them in the position where they have to make choices between their careers, financial stability and independence, and health care on the one hand, and the ability to ensure their child receives Canadian citizenship on the other (Bjorkquist at para. 161). The same choices and sacrifices did not need to made by their male partners. The law therefore had a differential impact based on sex. Consequently, the Court declared section 3(3)(a) unconstitutional and of no force or effect pursuant to section 52 of the Constitution Act, 1982. However, the declaration of invalidity was suspended for a period of six months to allow Parliament an opportunity to pass new legislation to cure the constitutional invalidity. On June 19, 2024, the Court granted an extension of the suspension of the declaration of invalidity until August 9, 2024, despite expressing significant concern over the slow progress of the legislation intended to remedy this important matter. Government Response and Bill C-71 On January 22, 2024, Immigration, Refugees and Citizenship Canada (IRCC) issued a statement that IRCC will not appeal the Court ruling. On 23 May 2024, the Government of Canada introduced new legislation, Bill C-71: An Act to amend the Citizenship Act (2024), which completed first reading on May 23, 2024 but has not yet passed or come into force. The legislation would do two things: Automatically confer citizenship to any person already born who would have been a citizen were it not for the first-generation limit; and Establish a new framework for citizenship by descent going forward that would allow for access to citizenship beyond the first generation based on a substantial connection to Canada. Accordingly, there are many people who are not currently citizens due to the first-generation rule who will automatically become citizens if the legislation passes as written. For these individuals, an application can be made for proof of Canadian citizenship in order to receive a Canadian citizenship certificate, with the proper documentation: Get proof of citizenship - Canada.ca Going forward, the proposed new legislation introduces a substantial connection test to allow a Canadian parent born abroad, who acquires Canadian citizenship by descent, to pass on citizenship to their child born abroad. To meet the substantial connection test, a Canadian parent born abroad would need to demonstrate they have accumulated at least 1,095 days of physical presence in Canada before the birth or adoption of the child. The 1,095 day requirement is cumulative, not in a five (5) year period preceding the birth.
